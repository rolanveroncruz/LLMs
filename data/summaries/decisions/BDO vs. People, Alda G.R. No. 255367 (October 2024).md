## Legal Document Summary: BDO vs. People & Alda

**Document:** G.R. No. 255367, October 02, 2024, *Banco De Oro Unibank, Inc. vs. The People of the Philippines, and Ruby O. Alda*

**Nature:** Decision regarding BDO's motion to intervene in a criminal case (Estafa through Misappropriation) on appeal to the Court of Appeals (CA).

**Key Provisions:**

*   **Article 315(1)(b) of the Revised Penal Code:** Estafa through misappropriation. This is the central criminal charge.
*   **Rule 19, Sections 1 & 2 of the Rules of Court:** Relate to intervention in civil cases.  Section 1 outlines the requirements for intervention (legal interest, no undue delay, protection of rights). Section 2 specifies time to intervene.
*   **Rule 110, Section 16 of the Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure:**  Allows the offended party to intervene by counsel in the prosecution of the offense where civil liability is instituted in the criminal action.
*   **Rule 111, Section 1 of the Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure:** Addresses the deemed institution of civil liability in criminal actions, unless waived, reserved, or separately instituted before the criminal action.
*   **Rule 124, Section 11 of the Rules of Court:**  Governs the CA's scope of judgment in appealed cases.
* **Bank-Depositor relationship:** Governed by provisions on simple loan (Art. 1980 of the Civil Code).

**Facts:**

*   Ruby Alda (accused) was found guilty by the RTC of Estafa for misappropriating over-credited funds from her BDO Fast Card account (â‚±46,829,806.14).
*   The RTC ordered Alda to pay BDO actual and moral damages, attorney's fees, and costs of suit.
*   Alda appealed the RTC decision.
*   The Office of the Solicitor General (OSG), representing the People, filed a Manifestation in Lieu of Appellee's Brief, recommending Alda's acquittal, arguing lack of jurisdiction, failure to prove essential elements of Estafa, and viewing the matter as a civil dispute stemming from an overpayment.
*   BDO, who acted as private prosecutor in the RTC, sought to intervene in the CA appeal, arguing that the OSG was not adequately representing the prosecution's interests and BDO's rights to civil indemnity.
*   The CA denied BDO's Motion for Intervention, arguing that it was untimely (should have been filed before the RTC judgment) and that BDO was not an indispensable party.

**Obligations and Liabilities:**

*   **Ruby Alda:**  Potentially criminally liable for Estafa under Article 315(1)(b) RPC and civilly liable to BDO for the misappropriated funds.
*   **Banco de Oro Unibank, Inc.:** Obligated to manage its systems to prevent erroneous transfers, but also has the right to seek recovery of misappropriated funds.

**Potential Risks and Ambiguous Clauses:**

*   **Jurisdiction:** The OSG argued that the RTC lacked jurisdiction because the withdrawals occurred in Dubai. This is a significant risk to the conviction.
*   **Criminal Intent:** A key element of Estafa is intent.  The OSG argued that fraudulent intent was not proven beyond a reasonable doubt.
*   **Debtor-Creditor Relationship:** The OSG characterization of the relationship as solely debtor-creditor may be contentious. BDO's argument is that this relationship only extends to the intended deposit amount and not the over-credited amount.
*   **Agreement not to prosecute:** The OSG stated that BDO did not categorically deny that there was an agreement between Ruby and the bank that should she return the money that she excessively withdrew, the bank would no longer press criminal charges against her.
*   **Timeliness of Intervention:** The CA's decision denying intervention hinges on a strict reading of the rules regarding the timing of intervention. The Supreme Court explicitly held that the rule can be relaxed and emphasized that the Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure has no specific period when the offended party may intervene.

**Relevant Case Law:**

*   ***Central Bank of the Philippines v. Citytrust Banking Corporation*** and ***Consolidated Bank and Trust Corporation v. Court of Appeals***:  Establish the debtor-creditor relationship between banks and depositors, but this case distinguishes between that relationship and funds transferred in error.
*   ***Executive Secretary v. Northeast Freight Forwarders, Inc.***:  Deals with the burden of proof regarding potential delays caused by intervention.
*   ***Director of Lands v. Court of Appeals***, ***Tahanan Development Corporation v. Court of Appeals***, ***Mago v. Court of Appeals***, and ***Pinlac v. Court of Appeals***:  Illustrate cases where intervention was allowed even after judgment or at the appellate level.
*   ***Neptune Metal Scrap Recycling, Inc. v. Manila Electric Company***: Addresses motion for intervention and whether strict and rigid application should be applied.
*   ***People v. Santiago***:  Outlines the limited role of a private complainant in a criminal case.
*   ***Rodriguez v. Ponferrada***: Clarifies private offended party in estafa suit and civil action.
*   ***Roa v. Segunda***: Addresses exceptions when the private offended party is no longer permitted to intervene.
*   ***Lim Tek Goan v. Yatco***: Addresses the right to institute after the termination of the criminal case.
*   ***Banal v. Tadeo***: Explains general basis of civil liability arising from crime.

**Supreme Court Decision:**

*   The Supreme Court **GRANTED** BDO's Petition, reversing the CA's denial of intervention.
*   The Supreme Court held that BDO has a direct, material, and immediate interest in the outcome of the case, especially the civil aspect of the case. It found that BDO has actual, material, direct, and immediate interest as to the civil aspect of the case to intervene before the appellate court, as the latter's judgment on the appeal will directly affect BDO.
*   The Supreme Court found that BDO's intervention would avoid multiplicity of suits and save the court's time and resources and recognized that BDO's intervention after trial is recognized by jurisprudence and allowed under the Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure.

**Statutory References:**

*   Revised Penal Code:  Article 315(1)(b) (Estafa)
*   Rules of Court: Rule 19, Sections 1 & 2
*   Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure: Rule 110, Section 16 and Rule 111, Section 1

**Ambiguous or Contentious Clauses and Arguments:**

*   **Nature of Funds:** The central dispute revolves around whether the over-credited funds were truly "received in trust" or simply the result of a banking error, impacting the Estafa charge.
*   **Role of VISA:** The OSG's reliance on VISA's approval of the transactions as a mitigating factor or evidence of a system glitch, rather than criminal intent, could be contentious.
*   **The weight and impact of OSG's Manifestation:** While the Supreme Court recognized the OSG's role, they emphasized that the CA has the power to reach its own independent decision.

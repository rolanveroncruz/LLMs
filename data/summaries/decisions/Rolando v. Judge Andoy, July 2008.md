**Case:** *Rolando V. Blanco v. Judge Teresito A. Andoy* (A.M. No. MTJ-08-1700, July 23, 2008)

**Summary:**
This is an administrative case against Judge Andoy for gross incompetence, gross misconduct, and violation of the New Code of Judicial Conduct. The charges stem from the handling of estafa and B.P. 22 cases filed against Blanco by Hemisphere Drug Corporation. The Supreme Court found Judge Andoy guilty of both undue delay in resolving a motion and gross ignorance of procedure, specifically concerning preliminary investigations.

**Key Provisions & Obligations:**

*   **Right to Speedy Disposition (Article VIII, Section 15(1) of the Constitution):** Lower courts must decide cases within three months.
*   **Rule 3.05, Code of Judicial Conduct:** Judges must dispose of court business promptly.
*   **Rule 112, Section 1, Rules of Court:** Preliminary investigation required for offenses where the penalty exceeds 4 years, 2 months, and 1 day.

**Liabilities:**

*   **Undue Delay:** Blanco's motion remained unresolved for over five months beyond the constitutional deadline.
*   **Gross Ignorance of Procedure:** Failure to conduct a preliminary investigation for estafa charges where required under Rule 112, despite the potential penalties involved.
*   The respondent judge was fined 25,000.00 for undue delay and gross ignorance of the law

**Potential Risks/Contentious Clauses:**

*   **Definition of "Undue Delay":** While the Constitution sets a 3-month deadline, case law may provide nuances on what constitutes a reasonable delay, especially considering the complexity of the motion and the court's workload. However, the Judge admitted that the Motion was submitted for resolution on January 16, 2007, and acted upon only on October 1, 2007.
*   **Proof of Malice/Corrupt Motive:** The Court acknowledged the lack of malicious intent, but still found the judge liable for gross ignorance. Demonstrating an absence of malice could mitigate penalties in similar cases.
*   **Mitigating Circumstances:** The Court considered the judge's pending retirement and that this was his first administrative offense of this nature.

**Analysis and Potential Arguments:**

*   The case highlights the importance of judges adhering to procedural rules, particularly regarding preliminary investigations. Even without malice, ignorance of basic legal principles can result in administrative liability.
*   The Supreme Court's decision emphasizes the right to a speedy disposition of cases and the consequences of prolonged delays.
*   Future cases involving judicial misconduct should consider the specific circumstances, including the judge's experience, caseload, and any mitigating factors, when determining the appropriate penalty.

## Legal Document Summary: *Saluday v. People* (G.R. No. 215305)

**Nature:** Petition for Review on Certiorari assailing a Court of Appeals decision affirming a Regional Trial Court conviction for illegal possession of high-powered firearm, ammunition, and explosives under Presidential Decree No. 1866, as amended.

**Key Issue:** Legality of the warrantless search of the petitioner's bag on a public bus, and the admissibility of the seized evidence (firearm, ammunition, and grenade).

**Ruling:** Petition DENIED. Conviction AFFIRMED.

**Key Provisions/Elements of the Offense (PD 1866):**

*   **Existence of the Firearm/Ammunition/Explosive:** Established by evidence and petitioner's own corroboration (presence of the bag, contents, and circumstances).
*   **Ownership/Possession:**  Court found actual or constructive possession, relying on petitioner's actions and statements.
*   **Lack of License:** Prosecution presented certification from the PNP Firearms and Explosives Division.

**Reasoning and Rationale:**

1.  **Reasonable Search (Reduced Expectation of Privacy):**  The Court characterized the bus inspection by Task Force Davao at a military checkpoint as a "reasonable search," based on the premise of a reduced expectation of privacy on public transportation. Applied to the case the Katz v. United States ruling. Cited People v. Johnson (airport searches), Dela Cruz v. People (seaport searches), and People v. Breis (bus searches).
2.  **Consented Search:** Court held that the petitioner gave free consent to the search. Based on petitioner's testimony, when SCAA Buco asked if he could open petitioner's bag, petitioner answered "yes, just open it" based on petitioner's own testimony. Cited, People v. Omaweng.
3.  **Warrantless Search:** The search on a public bus constituted a reasonable and consented search, rendering the warrant requirement inapplicable. Court provided guidelines for bus searches to protect rights of passengers on transit buses in the Philippines.

**Case Law and Statutory References:**

*   **PD 1866:**  Codifies laws on illegal possession of firearms and explosives.
*   **Section 2, Article III, Philippine Constitution:**  Guarantee against unreasonable searches and seizures.
*   **Fourth Amendment, US Constitution:** Patterned the constitutional provision under Section 2, Article III of the Constitution, providing historical context.
*   **Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967)**: Established the "reasonable expectation of privacy" test.
*   **People v. Johnson, 401 Phil. 734 (2000)**
*   **Dela Cruz v. People, 776 Phil. 653 (2016)**
*   **People v. Breis, 766 Phil. 785 (2015)**
*   **Fortune Express, Inc. v. Court of Appeals, 364 Phil. 480 (1999)**
*   **Costabella Corp. v. Court of Appeals, 271 Phil. 350 (1991)**
*   **People v. Asuncion, 362 Phil. 118, 127 (1999)**
*   **People v. Montilla, 349 Phil. 640, 661 (1998)**
*   **People v. Omaweng, 288 Phil. 350, 358-359 (1992)**
*   **Civil Code, Article 429.**

**Potential Risks/Contentious Points:**

*   **Reasonable Search vs. Warrantless Search:** The Court distinguishes between the two, but the line between them can be blurry.
*   **Scope of Consent:** The extent and voluntariness of the petitioner's consent to the search could be challenged, especially given the power dynamic between the individual and law enforcement.  
*   **Chain of Custody:** While the petitioner did not challenge the chain of custody in this case, future cases could raise this issue to challenge the integrity of the evidence seized.
*   **Application to Future Cases:** The guidelines provided for bus searches could be subject to varying interpretations and potential abuse by law enforcement if not carefully applied.

**Liabilities:**

*   Petitioner found guilty of Illegal possession of high powered firearm and ammunition. He is sentenced to suffer an imprisonment of prision mayor in its minimum period and is ordered to pay a fine of P30,000.00.

*   Petitioner found guilty of Illegal possession of explosive. He is sentenced to suffer an imprisonment of prision mayor in its maximum period to reclusion temporal and is ordered to pay a fine of P50,000.00.

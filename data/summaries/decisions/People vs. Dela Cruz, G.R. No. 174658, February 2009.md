**Legal Document Summary: *People of the Philippines vs. Marlon dela Cruz @ "Dagul," et al.* G.R. No. 174658, February 24, 2009 (599 Phil. 369)**

**Nature of the Case:** Appeal from a Court of Appeals decision affirming the conviction of Marlon Dela Cruz for (1) violation of Republic Act No. 6539 (Anti-Carnapping Law) and (2) Robbery with Homicide under Art. 294 of the Revised Penal Code.

**Key Provisions and Findings:**

*   **Circumstantial Evidence:** The Supreme Court upholds the conviction based on circumstantial evidence, emphasizing the requirements of Section 4, Rule 133 of the Rules of Court: (a) more than one circumstance; (b) facts proven; and (c) combination leads to conviction beyond reasonable doubt, excluding all others. *People v. Padua* cited.
*   **Anti-Carnapping Law (RA 6539):** Defined as taking, with intent to gain, of a motor vehicle belonging to another without consent, or by means of violence/intimidation. The conviction was upheld as the circumstances proved Dela Cruz took a motorcycle.
*   **Robbery with Homicide:** Elements outlined as: (1) taking of property with violence/intimidation; (2) property belongs to another; (3) *animus lucrandi*; (4) homicide committed by reason or on occasion of robbery. *People v. Cabbab, Jr.* cited.
*   **Extrajudicial Confession:** Anna Datlag's testimony about Dela Cruz's confession was deemed admissible because she testified in court and was cross-examined. Section 33, Rule 130, Rules of Court cited. *People v. Licayan* used for legal precedence. Testimony was corroborated by body injuries and motorcyle evidence.
*   **Penalties:** The Supreme Court upheld the Court of Appeals' sentencing. Dela Cruz was sentenced with *Reclusion Perpetua* for each case, as originally stated, but was modified to the indeterminate penalty of imprisonment of 17 years and four months as minimum to 30 years as maximum in order to follow through with the lack of aggravating circumstances during commission of the crime as alleged in the original information.

**Key Obligations and Liabilities:**

*   **Appellant Marlon Dela Cruz:** Held criminally liable for carnapping and robbery with homicide. Ordered to pay victim's wife for indemnity, moral damages, funeral/burial expenses, and temperate damages.

**Potential Risks and Contentious Clauses:**

*   **Reliance on Circumstantial Evidence:** Although upheld, the conviction heavily relies on circumstantial evidence. Defense counsel could potentially challenge the strength and coherence of the proven circumstances, arguing they do not definitively exclude other possible perpetrators or explanations.
*   **Assessment of Damages:** The amount of damages awarded was already modified by the Court of Appeals. Potential risk of challenge to the specific amounts awarded, despite the Court of Appeals modification and the subsequent affirmation by the Supreme Court.

**Statutory References:**

*   Republic Act No. 6539 (Anti-Carnapping Law)
*   Revised Penal Code, Article 294 (Robbery with Homicide)
*   Rules of Court, Rule 130, Section 33 (Admissibility of Confessions)
*   Rules of Court, Rule 133, Section 4 (Sufficiency of Circumstantial Evidence)

**Case Law References:**

*   *People v. Padua*, G.R. No. 169075, February 23, 2007
*   *People v. Cabbab, Jr.*, G.R. No. 173479, July 12, 2007
*   *People v. Licayan*, 428 Phil. 332 (2002).

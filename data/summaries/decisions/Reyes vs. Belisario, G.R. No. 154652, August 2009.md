## Legal Document Summary - Reyes v. Belisario & Malicdem (G.R. No. 154652)

**Document Type:** Supreme Court Decision (Petition for Review on Certiorari)

**Subject Matter:**  Appealability of an Ombudsman decision exonerating a public official (Petitioner Reyes) in an administrative case for oppression and harassment filed by Respondents Belisario and Malicdem.  The case stems from the reassignment of the Respondents shortly after they filed a graft complaint against the Petitioner. The CSC declared the reassignments as invalid, tainted with bad faith, and constituting constructive dismissal.

**Key Provisions:**

*   **Petitioner's Argument:** The Ombudsman's decision exonerating him is final and unappealable per Section 7, Rule III of the Rules of Procedure of the Office of the Ombudsman (Ombudsman Rules) and Section 27 of RA 6770 (Ombudsman Act).
*   **Respondents' Argument:**  The Ombudsman committed grave abuse of discretion in exonerating the Petitioner, justifying CA review.
*   **Supreme Court Ruling:** The petition is DENIED. The CA Decision and Resolution are affirmed with modification of the penalty.

**Obligations:**

*   While technically the Ombudsman's decision should have been final and unappealable under existing rules, the gravity of the situation warranted the CA to review the case.
*   Petitioner's primary obligation was to act within the bounds of the law regarding employee reassignments and avoid actions constituting harassment or oppression.

**Liabilities:**

*   The Supreme Court finds the Petitioner liable for oppression against the Respondents.

**Potential Risks/Contentious Clauses:**

*   **Ambiguity in Finality of Ombudsman Decisions:** The case highlights a potential ambiguity in applying Section 7, Rule III of the Ombudsman Rules, particularly when grave abuse of discretion is alleged. While the rule aims for finality, it cannot shield decisions made with grave abuse of discretion.
*   **Distinction between CSC and Ombudsman Jurisdiction:** The Court clarifies the separate jurisdictions of the CSC (personnel matters) and the Ombudsman (administrative sanctions).  While their investigations may overlap factually, they serve different purposes and lead to different outcomes.
*   **Presumption of Regularity:** The Court finds that the presumption of regularity cannot be applied to acts outside an official's legal authority, especially when those acts are within another entity's jurisdiction (the CSC's authority over personnel reassignments).
*   **Definition of Oppression:** The Court emphasizes that harassment and oppression can be a series of acts, not necessarily involving physical violence or intimidation, that prejudice the respondents.

**Case Law/Statutory References:**

*   **Section 3(e) of Republic Act No. 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act):** Referenced in the original complaint filed by the respondents.
*   **Republic Act No. 6770 (Ombudsman Act), Section 27:**  Cited by the Petitioner regarding the finality of Ombudsman decisions.
*   **Rules of Procedure of the Office of the Ombudsman (Ombudsman Rules), Section 7, Rule III:** Cited by the Petitioner regarding the finality of Ombudsman decisions.
*   **Rules of Court, Rule 43:** Initially invoked by Respondents for the Petition for Review, then treated as a Rule 65 Petition.
*   **Rules of Court, Rule 65 (Certiorari):**  Impliedly used by the CA to review the Ombudsman's decision based on grave abuse of discretion.
*   **CSC MC No. 40, s. 1998, Sec 6a:** Regarding employee reassignments.
*   **Executive Order No. 286, s. 1995, Section 3.1:** Regarding the authority of the LWUA Board of Trustees, not the Administrator, to order personnel reassignments.
*   **Executive Order No. 292 (Civil Service Law):** basis for finding the petitioner guilty of harassment and oppression.
*   **Fabian vs. Desierto, G.R No. 129742, September 16, 1998, 295 SCRA 470:** Respondent may appeal the orders, directives, or decisions of the Office of the Ombudsman to the Court of Appeals via a petition for review under Rule 43.
*   **Barata v. Abalos, Jr., G.R. No. 142888, June 6, 2001, 358 SCRA 575, 581:** If a sentence of censure, reprimand and a one-month suspension is considered final and unappealable, so should exoneration.
*   **Chan v. Ombudsman Marcelo, G.R. No.159298, July 6, 2007, 526 SCRA 627:** If a sentence of censure, reprimand and a one-month suspension is considered final and unappealable, so should exoneration.
*   **Ang Tibay v. CIR, 69 Phil. 635 (1940):** An administrative decision, in order to be valid, should have, among others, "something to support itself.

**Key Points for a Lawyer:**

*   The case confirms the limited appeal rights of complainants in Ombudsman administrative cases, but emphasizes that the courts can still intervene via certiorari when grave abuse of discretion exists.
*   The case demonstrates the importance of procedural fairness and adherence to jurisdictional boundaries in administrative proceedings.
*   A finding of "grave abuse of discretion" can nullify a decision that would otherwise be considered final and unappealable.
*   The CSC's findings regarding the legality of personnel actions (reassignments) have a significant impact on related administrative cases before the Ombudsman.
*   The court will look at the whole picture of actions constituting possible oppression and harassment, even if on their own they are not illegal acts.

**Outcome:**

The Court upheld the CA's decision, finding that the Ombudsman committed grave abuse of discretion, and modified the penalty to a one (1) year suspension (or a fine equivalent to one year's salary if the petitioner is no longer employed).

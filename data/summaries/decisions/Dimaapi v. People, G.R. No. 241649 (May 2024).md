**Legal Document Summary: *Ron De Guzman Dimaapi v. People of the Philippines* (G.R. No. 241649, May 22, 2024)**

**Nature of Document:** Decision of the Supreme Court of the Philippines resolving a Petition for Review on Certiorari regarding a conviction for Robbery in an Inhabited House under Article 299 of the Revised Penal Code (RPC).

**Key Provisions:**

*   **Article 299, RPC:** Defines and penalizes robbery in an inhabited house. Specifically, Article 299(a)(2) concerns entry by breaking a wall, roof, door or window.
*   **Circumstantial Evidence:**  The Court relies on circumstantial evidence to uphold the conviction, emphasizing that direct evidence isn't always necessary.  Referencing *People v. Lignes*, the Court reiterates the conditions for circumstantial evidence to suffice for conviction: more than one circumstance, proven acts, and the combination must produce conviction beyond a reasonable doubt.
*   **Definition of "Inhabited House" (Art. 301, RPC):** Any shelter, ship, or vessel constituting the dwelling of one or more persons.
*   **Indeterminate Sentence Law:** Applied in determining the penalty.

**Key Obligations & Liabilities:**

*   **Dimaapi's Liability:** Found guilty beyond reasonable doubt of robbery in an inhabited house. Sentenced to an indeterminate prison term of 8 years and 1 day of *prision mayor* (minimum) to 14 years, 8 months, and 1 day of *reclusion temporal* (maximum). Liable for actual damages of PHP 55,000 to the victim, Zenaida Angara, representing the value of stolen cash and goods.
*   **Civil Liability:**  Ordered to pay PHP 55,000 in actual damages, with 6% legal interest per annum from the finality of the decision until full payment.

**Potential Risks & Contentious Clauses:**

*   **Reliance on Circumstantial Evidence:** While the Court provides strong justification, reliance on circumstantial evidence is always subject to scrutiny, particularly regarding whether the chain of circumstances definitively excludes all reasonable hypotheses of innocence. The defense may try to argue that the inference drawn is not airtight.
*   **Determining intent to gain:** The defendant can argue that the prosecution did not effectively determine that the circumstances of the robbery indicated an intention to gain.
*   **Armed element:** The RPC prescribes a higher penalty for armed persons. The defendant may argue that possession of the weapons does not constitute an armed person during the event.
*   **Valuation of Stolen Goods:** The amount of property stolen affects the penalty. The defense could potentially challenge the valuation of the cash and cigarettes (PHP 55,000) if it could be shown to be inaccurate.
*   **Precedent Questioned:** The Court distinguishes this case from *People v. Tubog*, addressing a potential issue regarding the lack of specific allegations about the dwelling place in the Information. This requires close examination to ensure the *Tubog* distinction is valid.
*   **Possible challenges of double jeopardy:** The defendant can argue that the other elements of the crime did not indicate the defendant was involved.

**Case Law & Statutory References:**

*   **Revised Penal Code (RPC):** Articles 299 (Robbery in an Inhabited House) and 301 (Definition of Inhabited House).
*   **Rules of Court, Rule 133, Section 4:**  Addresses sufficiency of circumstantial evidence for conviction.
*   ***People v. Lignes*:** Reinforces the use of circumstantial evidence in criminal convictions.
*   ***People v. Modesto*:** Discusses the standard for appreciating circumstantial evidence.
*   ***Tumog v. People*:**  Outlines the elements necessary for a conviction of robbery in an inhabited house.
*   ***People v. Tubog*:** Relates to the requirement that the Information specifically allege that the store of the private complainant was also being used as a dwelling place
